Rezio TIX —
OMO Ticketing

A 0-to-1 software + hardware system that extended Rezio from online booking into on-site ticketing and payment — grounded in hands-on user research and built together with a designer.

Role: Product Manager 2021–2022 Rezio (a KKday company)
↓ ~30% operating cost for adopting venues
Context & Problem

Online and offline lived in two separate worlds

Rezio began as a purely online booking platform. But attractions and theme parks with physical entrances still ran their on-site operations on manual ticketing, paper tickets and a patchwork of point-of-sale hardware. That created three pain points:

  • Online and offline systems were siloed — orders and inventory data couldn't be unified.
  • On-site selling, cash handling, invoicing and end-of-day closing relied on manual work — slow and error-prone.
  • Paper tickets and scattered checkout devices made the on-site flow and reconciliation cumbersome.

Venues needed one solution that connected seamlessly to their existing online system while also covering the realities of the physical front line.

Goal

One OMO solution for on-site sales

Build Rezio TIX — an OMO (online-merge-offline) on-site ticketing solution. The scope I owned was on-site sales:

POS counter machine

Staff-assisted selling, cash and multi-payment, invoice issuance and end-of-day closing.

Self-service kiosk

Visitors buy and pay on their own, with no staff assistance.

Both share the same trip and inventory data as the existing online system, achieving true online-offline integration.

User Research

Researching a product with no precedent

This was Rezio's first move into physical ticketing, with no internal playbook to lean on — so I led the user research myself and used it as the basis for product and design decisions:

  • In-depth interviews with people who had hands-on ticketing / cashiering experience, to understand the real on-site pain points and habits.
  • Competitive research: ticket machines were rare to study, so I studied mature self-checkout and self-ordering kiosks instead, borrowing their self-service, payment and error-prevention patterns.
  • Interactive prototype testing: ran user research on a clickable prototype to validate the flow and find friction before development.
  • Ongoing interviews after the DEMO: once a demo version shipped, I kept interviewing prospective clients to iterate on real feedback before and after launch.

The research surfaced three roles with very different contexts; I handed those insights to the designer so the design could answer real needs:

🧑‍💼 Counter ticketing staff (POS)

Trained, but working under queue pressure — they need to sell and take payment fast and without mistakes. → Minimize taps, default to the most likely choice, and block errors before checkout.

🧍 Self-service visitors (Kiosk)

Untrained, possibly older or international, using it just once. → The flow must be self-explanatory with zero learning — linear steps, obvious labels and icons.

🧮 Manager / accountant (back office)

Needs trustworthy reconciliation and traceable cancel / refund records. → Closing forces discrepancy reasons; cancel and refund leave a full audit trail.

✦ The core idea: research "on-site selling" as a product with real users — not just port the online flow onto a machine.
Solution · Product & Flow Design

Designed for the front line

I defined the flows below from the research insights and shaped them together with the designer, who owned the visual interface.

A POS flow designed for speed

Because counter staff sell under queue pressure, the interface optimizes for efficiency and error prevention:

  • Smart date / session selection: defaults to today's sessions; a "next session date" button jumps straight to the next date that actually has sessions (not just tomorrow); a 3-month calendar picker; horizontal swiping when there are many sessions; sessions past their cutoff are hidden automatically to prevent mis-selling.
  • Intuitive cart: adjust quantity with +/− or tap the number field to type directly; "remove" only appears after decrementing to 1, preventing accidental deletion; the cart summary groups identical trips together for a clean final check with the customer.
  • Easy cash handling: type the tendered amount on a number pad and change is auto-calculated; "Confirm" stays disabled until tendered ≥ amount due — preventing payment errors at the interface level.
  • Flexible voucher printing: print on the spot, or choose "print later" and reprint from order lookup.

A Kiosk flow designed for zero learning

The kiosk faces one-time visitors with no one to help, so the flow is deliberately linear and self-explanatory — letting travelers of any age or language complete purchase and payment on their own and take pressure off the counter.

Rigorous fail-safes (a UX design focus)

  • Daily cash reconciliation & closing: count cash by denomination, auto-calculate, and compare against the expected drawer total — any mismatch requires a written reason before closing, with manager-level authentication.
  • Cancel and refund separated: two independent, non-interfering actions; every record, reason and internal note flows back to the Admin back office, so accounting stays traceable.
  • Pre-checkout auto-validation: the cart checks inventory and minimum-order limits before payment; an authorized user can still force-create when needed — balancing flexibility and control.
  • Seat-hold mechanism: orders hold inventory for 10 minutes after checkout and auto-expire if unpaid, so stock isn't locked up.

Standardized global rollout

I personally wrote the product manuals and SOPs — documenting POS / Kiosk setup, selling, lookup, refund and closing — so teams and clients across regions could onboard with a consistent process and lower coordination cost.

Highlights

What made this hard — and interesting

Research-driven, not gut-driven

With no ticket machine to copy, I leaned on interviews, competitive research (self-checkout / self-ordering kiosks) and interactive prototype testing to pin down the gap between a trained cashier who needs speed and a one-time visitor who needs zero learning — then handed those insights to the designer to execute.

0→1 software + hardware integration

Unlike a pure software feature, this project spanned hardware devices, payment, invoicing and the online system — aligning specs across multiple vendors, then distilling complex on-site operations into a clean, easy-to-operate terminal interface.

Agile response to vendor risk

During 2021 production testing, the original invoice / payment hardware vendor underperformed. To win the Heping Island Geopark bid, the team decisively switched to a new hardware partner, re-ran compatibility development and integration testing — and still shipped on schedule.

Treating on-site operations as a product

Many decisions — from cash-count discrepancy control to the separation of cancel and refund — came from understanding how front-line ticketing and accounting staff actually work, so the system doesn't just "sell tickets" but genuinely reduces on-site errors and reconciliation load.
